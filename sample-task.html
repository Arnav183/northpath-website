The task

Every task starts from a persona with a real question and a document set that contains the answer in pieces. The documents below hold two buried complications. A strong response has to find both.

persona

Owner of a small retail bakery. One operating account, one card processor, one equipment loan. Not an accountant, and does not want to become one.

prompt

My processor says I made $18,240 in March but my bank shows a lot less coming in. Where did the difference go, and am I still meeting the deposit requirement in my loan?

Two model responses

The same prompt and documents go to two models. Read both. One contains three verifiable failures, and every one of them sounds reasonable until you check the documents.

response A

Good news: nothing is missing. Your processor collected $18,240 and paid out $17,399 after $841 in processing costs, so the difference your bank shows is just those costs. Your processor agreement caps chargebacks at 1 percent, so nothing else is being withheld. On the loan: your March earnings of $17,399 are above the $16,500 floor in Section 4.2, so you remain comfortably compliant. No action needed.

response B

Three things explain the gap. Your processor collected $18,240, deducted $529 in fees and $312 in chargebacks, and paid out $17,399 across five batches. The final batch of $1,205 settled April 1, so your March bank deposits total $16,194. On the loan: Section 4.2 measures deposits into the account, and $16,194 is below the $16,500 floor. If your lender counts the April 1 settlement as March activity, you clear it at $17,399. Confirm the measurement basis with your lender in writing before this becomes a rate conversation.

  • Incomplete reconciliation. The bank gap is $2,046, not $841. Response A explains fees and chargebacks but never notices that the bank total is $16,194, because the final $1,205 batch settled in April.
  • Fabricated clause. No processor agreement was provided, and no document mentions a chargeback cap. The claim is also false on its own terms: $312 of chargebacks on $18,240 of sales is 1.7 percent.
  • Wrong basis, unsupported reassurance. Section 4.2 measures deposits into the account, not earnings. On deposits, March is $16,194 and the covenant is breached. Response A reassures the owner using a number the covenant does not use.

The rubric

Responses are graded against weighted criteria written before any response is read. Each criterion has pass and fail anchors, so two raters grading independently should land on the same score. Note that the rubric was built from the documents, which is why it can catch failures a casual read would miss.

criterionanchorsweight
Reconciles gross sales to bank deposits exactly pass: fees, chargebacks, and settlement timing all identified, and the $2,046 gap fully accounted for. fail: any unexplained remainder. 3
Arithmetic verified against the documents pass: every stated figure recomputable from the documents. fail: any value that does not check out. 3
Cites only figures and clauses that exist pass: every reference maps to a provided document. fail: any invented clause, cap, or number. 3
Applies the covenant's actual measurement basis pass: tests Section 4.2 against deposits into the account. fail: substitutes earnings, payouts, or gross sales. 3
Flags the settlement-timing gray area pass: notes that the April 1 settlement changes the covenant answer and says how to resolve it. fail: silently picks one reading. 2
Answers both questions with a next action pass: explains the gap, addresses the covenant, and tells the owner what to do. 1

The judgment

Each response is graded per criterion, then the pair gets a preference direction, a strength, and a written justification. Nothing ships until the math is recomputed, every citation is checked, and the covenant language is reread.

preference: B · strength 3 of 3

A reconciles only to the processor's net figure and never explains the bank total, invents a chargeback cap that appears in no document, and tests the covenant against earnings instead of deposits. B ties every number across all three documents, reads Section 4.2 the way a lender would, and turns the gray area into a concrete next step. A's arithmetic passes because its own figures recompute; the failure is in what it never checked.
